Automated Reverse-Engineering of Audio into Generative Music Prompts: A Technical Framework for aisonggenerator.io
1. Introduction
1.1 The Paradigm Shift in Music Creation
The domain of music production is currently undergoing a seismic shift, precipitated by the maturation of generative artificial intelligence. We are transitioning from a paradigm of manual assembly—where producers manipulate MIDI data and audio waveforms within a Digital Audio Workstation (DAW)—to a paradigm of conditional generation. In this new era, the primary interface for creation is not the piano keyboard or the mixing console, but the text prompt. Platforms such as aisonggenerator.io, alongside contemporaries like Suno and Udio, have demonstrated the capability to synthesize high-fidelity musical compositions from textual descriptions alone.1 These systems utilize advanced architectures, likely variants of transformers or diffusion models, to map semantic language inputs to complex acoustic outputs.
However, this technological leap has introduced a novel friction point for creators: the "Linguistic-Auditory Gap." While the generative models are highly capable, the interface remains bound by the user's ability to articulate auditory concepts into text. A user listening to a reference track—perhaps a demo they recorded or a commercial release they wish to emulate—often lacks the precise vocabulary to describe the intricate interplay of timbre, rhythm, and structure that defines the song's "vibe." They hear a "driving, dark pulse," but the AI requires specific tags like "Synthwave," "120 BPM," "Analog Bass," and "Minor Key" to reproduce it accurately. This disconnect results in a trial-and-error workflow that is inefficient and often frustrating.
1.2 Research Objective and Scope
This report proposes a comprehensive technical solution to bridge this gap: an Automated Audio-to-Prompt Analysis Tool. The objective is to engineer a software pipeline that ingests a raw audio file (WAV or MP3) and algorithmically "reverse-engineers" it into a song template optimized for aisonggenerator.io.
This tool is not merely a file converter; it is an intelligent interrogator. It must deconstruct a mix into its constituent elements—identifying the genre from spectral characteristics, extracting the tempo from transient patterns, detecting instrumentation through deep learning classification, and mapping the song's arrangement (Intro, Verse, Chorus) using structural segmentation. The final output is a structured text block (the "Prompt") that a user can directly input into aisonggenerator.io to generate a new, original composition that mirrors the stylistic and structural fingerprint of the reference audio.
1.3 Technical Methodology
The proposed framework leverages a "best-in-class" approach, integrating multiple specialized Python libraries for Music Information Retrieval (MIR).
* Librosa: Utilized for fundamental signal processing, including loading audio, spectral analysis (STFT, Mel-spectrograms), and recurrence matrix calculation.4
* Essentia (TensorFlow): Employed for high-level semantic analysis. We specifically leverage Essentia's pre-trained TensorFlow models, which have been trained on massive datasets like MTG-Jamendo, to perform multi-label classification of genres, moods, and instruments.6
* All-In-One (AllIn1): Selected for its state-of-the-art capability in Music Structure Analysis. Unlike general segmentation algorithms, AllIn1 uses transformer models to explicitly label sections (e.g., "Verse," "Chorus"), which is a critical requirement for the aisonggenerator.io template format.7
* Madmom: Integrated for its superior performance in beat and downbeat tracking using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), ensuring precise tempo estimation for the prompt.8
This report will detail the architecture of this system, from the theoretical underpinnings of audio feature extraction to the specific formatting requirements of the target platform.

3. Theoretical Foundations of Audio Feature Extraction
Before detailing the implementation, it is necessary to establish the theoretical basis for how we extract "meaning" from "signal." Audio is natively represented as a time-series of amplitude values (samples). To extract genre, mood, and structure, we must transform this data into representations that expose latent musical features.
3.1 Time-Frequency Representations
The foundational step in our analysis pipeline is the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT). This operation decomposes the audio signal into its constituent frequencies over time, creating a Spectrogram.14
* Mechanism: We slice the audio into short windows (typically 20-30ms) and apply the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) to each window.
* Significance: This reveals the energy distribution across the frequency spectrum. A heavy presence of low frequencies (20-100Hz) suggests a "Bass-heavy" style. A chaotic distribution of high frequencies might indicate "Noise" or "Distortion."
3.2 Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs)
For timbre analysis (e.g., distinguishing a guitar from a piano), the raw spectrogram is often too high-dimensional and perceptually linear. We utilize MFCCs, which compress the spectral envelope into a representation that mimics human hearing (the Mel scale).16
* Relevance: MFCCs are the standard input for many classical machine learning models used in genre classification and instrument recognition. They effectively capture the "color" of the sound, independent of pitch.
3.3 Chroma Features and the Tonnetz Space
To analyze harmony (Chords, Key, Mood), we use Chroma Features. This representation projects the entire spectrum onto the 12 semitones of the musical octave, ignoring octave differences.16
* Application: By analyzing the evolution of Chroma vectors over time, we can determine the key (Major vs. Minor) and the chord progression.
* Tonnetz: A further transformation into Tonnetz space maps pitch classes onto a geometric grid (hypertorus), which is particularly effective for detecting harmonic stability and mood (e.g., distinguishing "Happy/Consonant" from "Sad/Dissonant").18
3.4 Deep Embeddings
Modern MIR has moved beyond handcrafted features (like MFCCs) to Deep Embeddings. These are the output vectors from the penultimate layers of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained on massive audio datasets (like AudioSet or Jamendo).
* VGGish / OpenL3 / MusiCNN: These models learn to extract complex, non-linear features that correlate with high-level concepts like "Groove," "Aggression," or specific instrument timbres.6
* Usage: Our tool will primarily rely on these embeddings via the Essentia library, as they offer superior performance for semantic tagging compared to raw signal processing.

4. The "Interrogator" Pipeline: Architecture and Implementation
The core of this report is the "Interrogator" pipeline—a Python-based architecture designed to systematically extract the necessary data points for aisonggenerator.io. The pipeline is divided into three sequential modules: Rhythmic Analysis, Semantic Classification, and Structural Segmentation.
4.1 Module A: Rhythmic and Temporal Analysis
The first requirement for a song template is the Tempo (BPM). This sets the grid for the AI generation.
4.1.1 The Challenge of Beat Tracking
Standard DSP methods for beat tracking (based on onset strength envelopes) often suffer from "Octave Errors"—identifying the tempo as double (e.g., 140 BPM detected as 280) or half (e.g., 140 detected as 70) the true value. For a prompt, 70 BPM and 140 BPM yield vastly different results (e.g., a slow ballad vs. a fast dubstep track).
4.1.2 Solution: Madmom vs. Librosa
While Librosa is capable (librosa.beat.beat_track), research indicates that Madmom offers superior performance for complex music.8
* Librosa Approach: Uses dynamic programming on an onset strength envelope. It is fast and lightweight but prone to errors in genres with syncopation (Jazz, Funk).
* Madmom Approach: Utilizes Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) trained on beat-annotated datasets. It "listens" to the context of the rhythm, making it far more robust against syncopation and dynamic tempo changes.
Implementation Strategy:
We will implement a consensus mechanism.
1. Extract Primary BPM: Use madmom.features.beats.RNNBeatProcessor to estimate the beat grid.
2. Extract Secondary BPM: Run librosa.beat.beat_track as a fallback.
3. Heuristic Validation: If the two values differ significantly, favor the Madmom output. Additionally, map the BPM to standard genre conventions (e.g., if Genre is "House," constrain BPM to 115-130).
Output for Template:
The tool generates a string: "120 BPM". This is appended to the Style Prompt.
4.2 Module B: Semantic Classification (The "Style" Prompt)
This module is responsible for generating the comma-separated list of adjectives, genres, and instruments required by aisonggenerator.io.9
4.2.1 Leveraging Essentia and TensorFlow
We employ the Essentia library, specifically its TensorFlow integration (essentia-tensorflow), which provides access to pre-trained models. The decision to use Essentia over training a custom model is driven by the availability of models trained on the MTG-Jamendo dataset.6
Why MTG-Jamendo?
The MTG-Jamendo Dataset is a massive collection of 55,000+ full audio tracks annotated with 195 tags across genre, instrument, and mood/theme categories.23 The vocabulary of this dataset (e.g., "synthesizer," "melancholic," "rock") aligns almost perfectly with the prompt requirements of generative AI models.
4.2.2 Sub-Module B1: Genre Classification
* Model: genre_discogs400-discogs-effnet-1.6
* Architecture: This model uses an EfficientNet architecture (a highly efficient CNN) trained on the Discogs dataset.
* Capabilities: It can predict 400 granular genre tags. Instead of just "Electronic," it can distinguish "Synthwave," "Techno," "Deep House," or "Ambient".24
* Implementation:
   1. Input audio is resampled to 16kHz.
   2. The model outputs a probability vector for 400 classes.
   3. Thresholding: We select the top 3-5 tags with a confidence score > 0.5.
* Mapping: The output tags (e.g., electronic---synthwave) are cleaned to Synthwave.
4.2.3 Sub-Module B2: Mood and Atmosphere
* Model: mtg_jamendo_moodtheme-musicnn-1.6
* Architecture: Uses a MusiCNN architecture, designed specifically for music tagging with convolutional filters that capture timbral and temporal patterns.
* Tag Set: 59 tags including "happy," "dark," "epic," "melancholic," "film," "space," "emotional".23
* Relevance: These tags map directly to the "Moods" section of the aisonggenerator.io style list.9 A detection of "film" and "epic" maps to the prompt Cinematic, Epic.
4.2.4 Sub-Module B3: Instrumentation Detection
Identifying instruments is critical for defining the sound palette.
* Model: mtg_jamendo_instrument-musicnn-1.6
* Classes: This model detects 41 specific instruments.
   * String: acousticguitar, electricguitar, violin, cello, strings.
   * Keys: piano, synthesizer, organ, harmonium.
   * Percussion: drums, drummachine, percussion.
   * Wind: saxophone, flute, brass.6
* Granularity: This allows the prompt to be specific. Instead of "Rock Band," the tool can specify "Electric Guitar, Bass, Drums, Synthesizer."
4.2.5 Fallback / Augmentation: OpenL3 and YAMNet
In cases where Essentia's confidence is low, we can employ OpenL3 or YAMNet.
* OpenL3: Generates robust audio embeddings that can be used to train a lightweight classifier (like an SVM) for specific instrument families if higher granularity is needed.19
* YAMNet: A model trained on AudioSet that detects 521 audio events.28 While it includes non-musical sounds, it is excellent for detecting specific textures like "Distortion," "Reverb," or niche instruments like "Tabla" or "Banjo" that might be absent from the Jamendo dataset.
4.3 Module C: Structural Segmentation (The "Lyrics" Template)
This is the most technically demanding module. The goal is to produce a map of the song: [Intro] -> [Verse 1] -> [Chorus] -> [Verse 2] -> [Chorus] -> [Outro].
4.3.1 The Limitation of Unsupervised Segmentation
Traditional approaches using Librosa rely on Self-Similarity Matrices (SSM).29
* Process: We compute MFCCs for every frame and compare every frame to every other frame.
* Visualization: This creates a matrix where diagonal lines off the main diagonal indicate repetitions (e.g., a Chorus repeating).
* Laplacian Segmentation: Algorithms can cluster these repetitions into "Segment A," "Segment B," etc..31
* The Problem: The algorithm does not know that "Segment A" is a "Verse." It only knows it is a distinct, repeating block. Mapping "A" to "Verse" requires complex, error-prone heuristics (e.g., assuming the most energetic repeating section is the Chorus).
4.3.2 The Solution: All-In-One (AllIn1)
To generate a professional template, we require Supervised Structural Analysis. The All-In-One (AllIn1) library is the state-of-the-art solution for this.7
* Architecture: AllIn1 uses a Transformer-based model trained on annotated structural datasets.
* Capabilities: It performs beat tracking, downbeat tracking, and—crucially—Functional Segment Labeling.
* Output: It explicitly labels sections as intro, verse, chorus, bridge, outro, and instrumental.7
* Precision: It provides start and end timestamps for each section.
Implementation Workflow:
1. Inference: result = allin1.analyze(audio_path)
2. Extraction: segments = result.segments
3. Data Structure:
JSON

 {"label": "intro", "start": 0.0, "end": 15.0},
 {"label": "verse", "start": 15.0, "end": 45.0},
 {"label": "chorus", "start": 45.0, "end": 75.0}
]

4. Refinement: We calculate the RMS Energy (using Librosa) for each segment. If the "Chorus" segment has significantly higher energy than the "Verse," we append the directive [Chorus: High Energy] or [Chorus: Powerful] to the template, leveraging the advanced prompting capabilities of the target platform.11

5. Synthesis: Constructing the Prompt
The final stage is the "Synthesis Engine," which translates the raw data from Modules A, B, and C into the text format required by aisonggenerator.io.
5.1 Style Field Synthesis
The platform requires a comma-separated string. The engine aggregates the highest-confidence tags.
Logic:
   1. Genre: Take the top 1-2 tags from genre_discogs400. (e.g., "Synthwave").
   2. Mood: Take the top 2-3 tags from mtg_jamendo_moodtheme. (e.g., "Nocturnal, Melancholic").
   3. Instruments: Take all tags from mtg_jamendo_instrument with confidence > 0.3. (e.g., "Synthesizer, Drum Machine, Bass").
   4. Tempo: Append the Madmom BPM estimate. (e.g., "120 BPM").
   5. Formatting: Capitalize and join with commas.
Resulting Prompt:
"Synthwave, Electronic, Nocturnal, Melancholic, Synthesizer, Drum Machine, Analog Bass, 120 BPM"
Advanced Heuristic: Style Mapping
The tool should use a dictionary to map technical tags to "market-friendly" tags used in the platform's dropdowns.9
   * electricguitar -> "Electric Guitar"
   * drum_machine -> "Drum Machine"
   * female_voice -> "Female Vocals"
   * high_arousal (from mood analysis) -> "Energetic"
5.2 Structural Template Synthesis
This generates the text for the "Lyrics" box.
Logic:
   1. Iterate through the segments list from AllIn1.
   2. Format the label as a meta-tag: [Label].
   3. Apply capitalization: intro -> [Intro].
   4. Insert newlines between sections to create a readable map.
   5. Optional Lyrics: Since the tool does not transcribe lyrics (unless integrated with Whisper), it inserts a placeholder [Insert Lyrics Here] under Vocal sections (Verse, Chorus) and omits it for Intro/Outro/Bridge if they are instrumental.
Resulting Template:

[Verse 1]
[Insert Lyrics Here]

[Insert Lyrics Here]
[Verse 2]
[Insert Lyrics Here]

General Advice:
For best results with v3.5, create only one or two sections at a time and then use the "Extend" feature.
If generating an entire song at once, experiment with alternative structural tags (see below) as Suno can get confused with too many standard "verse" and "chorus" tags.

Examples: [Intro], [Verse 1], [Pre-chorus], [Chorus], [Bridge], [Solo], [Outro], [Fade Out]

V. Extending Songs & Multi-Part Compositions
Iterative Process: Generate your first part. If you like a segment (e.g., 00:00 - 00:40), you can "Continue from this song" starting slightly before the end of the good part (e.g., from 00:25) to create the next section.
Short Segments: Even short generated samples (e.g., 20 seconds) can be valuable if they progress the song well. Add them to the full track and try extending from there.
Listen Holistically: Continuously listen to the full song to ensure new parts fit cohesively.
Quality Considerations:
Song quality may degrade with many parts. Suno generally performs best with 1-2 part songs extended.
A "hiss noise" can become more prominent with 6-7+ parts.
Uniqueness: The more parts a song has (if managed well), the higher the chance of creating something unique. This can involve changing rhythm, vocalists, and adding solos.
Credit Expectation: Creating a high-quality, unique song can often take 500-1000 credits.
VI. General Tips & Tricks
Embrace AI's Creativity: Focus on whether you like the output, even if it deviates from your exact prompt. Some of the best results occur when the AI takes creative liberties, especially further into a song (these are sometimes called "hallucinations" by users).
Genre Mashing: Experiment with creating sub-genres by combining multiple styles in your prompt.
Negative Prompts: Suno does not seem to effectively process negative prompts. For instance, no autotune might be ignored or even interpreted as a request for autotune.
Bracket Consistency: If you have a chorus in Part 1 with specific bracketed instructions and want to replicate it, use the exact same brackets. For a different chorus, use different brackets or no brackets, and adjust your style tags.
Simplicity is Key: The more complex your request, the more likely Suno is to struggle.
Post-Production: Consider using software like Audacity to edit, crop, and finalize your tracks.
